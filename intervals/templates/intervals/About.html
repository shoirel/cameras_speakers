{% endblock %} {% block content %} {% autoescape off %} 'It is typically found in such studies that much less than 90% of the intervals cover the actual number. Of course, in some cases it can be bad luck, but given that this happens systematically, it is interpreted as what scholars call “overprecision”: many people tend to overestimate the precision of their judgment. This tendency has important consequences for the quality of decision-making in many domains. For example, it might make you underdiversify your investment portfolio ("put all your eggs in the same basket"). If you are managing a project, you may underestimate the likelihood that it ends up taking much more time and money than expected. See e.g., Overprecision in Judgment (Moore et al.) to learn more (in particular, see section Ecological Evidence of Overprecision for some examples of real-life consequences of overprecision). For example, you will learn about the advantages and limitations of the kind of study you have just participated in. Interestingly, many people guess that less than 90% of their intervals will cover the true values, probably intuiting their own overprecision.
